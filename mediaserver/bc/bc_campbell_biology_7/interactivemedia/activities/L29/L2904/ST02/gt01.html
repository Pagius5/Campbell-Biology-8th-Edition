A haploid fern spore settles on moist ground. It germinates and develops into a tiny, heart-shaped haploid gametophyte that sustains itself by photosynthesis. Archegonia, the structures that produce eggs, develop on the underside of the gametophyte. Antheridia, also located on the underside of the gametophyte, produce sperm. Since the gametophyte is haploid, eggs and sperm are produced by mitosis. Sperm swim through moisture from an antheridium to an archegonium-- usually on another gametophyte. An egg is fertilized, forming a diploid zygote. The zygote grows into the large, conspicuous, diploid sporophyte that we identify as a fern. Spots called sori on the undersides of fern leaves are actually clusters of sporangia. In a sporangium, diploid cells undergo meiosis to produce haploid spores. The spores settle on the ground and develop into a new generation of fern gametophytes.
