CAM plants do not have the anatomical adaptations of C4 plants. In fact, their anatomy is comparable to C3 plants.

The CAM adaptation is to fix carbon dioxide at different times rather than in different locations. In CAM plants the stomata are closed during the day.

How do CAM plants obtain carbon dioxide if they keep their stomata closed?

They open their stomata at night to allow carbon dioxide to enter the plant. By only opening the stomata at night, when evaporation is the lowest and relative humidity is the highest, CAM metabolism is the most water-efficient of the types of photosynthesis.

Once inside the plant, the carbon dioxide must be stored for later use. CAM plants fix carbon dioxide into four–carbon molecules, just like C4 plants. These four–carbon molecules, such as malic acid, are stored in the vacuoles of the cell while the guard cells are open at night.

Once the sun rises, the guard cells close the stomata, and no more carbon dioxide will enter or leave the plant.

During the day, the stored four–carbon molecules will be broken down to release carbon dioxide to be used in photosynthesis.
