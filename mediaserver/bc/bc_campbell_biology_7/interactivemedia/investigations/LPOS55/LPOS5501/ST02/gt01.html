Historically, about 400,000 square miles of North America were composed of tall-grass prairie. Today, due to urbanization and agriculture, less than 1% of this important ecosystem remains.

Not only are prairies appreciated for their beauty and expansive views, but also prairies are useful ecosystems. The deep root systems of prairie plants stabilize soil, facilitate water filtration, and increase the recharge of groundwater. Prairies provide areas for breeding, feeding, nesting, and brood rearing for animals. In addition, rich soils associated with prairie ecosystems created the region of the U.S. referred to as the "breadbasket of the world."
