But there is still more to investigate. Given Mrs. B.'s behavioral changes, you suspect that her brain may be affected. To find out, you take a PET (positron emission tomography) scan, which enables physicians to determine patterns of blood flow in various organs, especially the brain and heart, without invading the body.

3. Use your Lab Notebook to note specific differences in the anatomy of the two brains.

- Can you see a noticeable size difference in the large, white, X-shaped structure at the center of each brain? This structure is one of the brain's ventricles, or spaces, called the caudate body.
- Another area, called the caudate nucleus, mediates among various sensory systems and many motor responses that affect muscle movements, emotions, and some cognitive (thinking) behaviors. The caudate nucleus is smaller in Mrs. B.'s brain than in the healthy brain. This is difficult to see; it appears as a white cloud nestled into the sides of the large, white, central X.
