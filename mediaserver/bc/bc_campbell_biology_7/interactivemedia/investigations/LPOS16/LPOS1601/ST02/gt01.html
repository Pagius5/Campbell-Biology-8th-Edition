The three models predicted different distributions of parental and new DNA strands. Matthew Meselson and Frank Stahl developed a way to distinguish the strands, using a heavy isotope of nitrogen, 15N. Replacing the normal 14N isotope with 15N produces heavier DNA. If the parental DNA contains 15N and the new DNA contains 14N, different combinations of parental and new DNA will have different densities.

To label the DNA, Meselson and Stahl first cultured bacteria in a growth medium containing 15N-labeled ammonium salt. All the DNA made in this medium contained the heavy 15N. They then shifted the bacteria to a growth medium containing 14N so that all new DNA made after the shift contained 14N. Culture samples were taken at the time of the shift, after replication cycle 1, and after replication cycle 2.
