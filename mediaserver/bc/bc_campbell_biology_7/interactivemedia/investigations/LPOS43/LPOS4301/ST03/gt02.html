These lesions are the patient's own cells growing inappropriately, invading normal tissue, and disrupting its structure; in other words, a cancer. This type is called Kaposi's sarcoma. A sarcoma is a tumor of solid tissues.

Think about the relationship between the Kaposi's sarcoma cells and HIV. How does HIV stimulate the growth of the tumor? This is an indirect effect. The tumor cells grow in an HIV-infected person not because the virus directly acts on the person's cells causing them to become cancerous, but because the virus kills the white blood cells whose job it is to recognize newly-formed cancer cells and kill them.

This cancer of the blood vessels that line the skin is rare in the general population, but it affects up to 70% of men with AIDS. Physicians can now eradicate these tumors with high success using chemotherapy with agents such as bleomycin, which interferes with DNA synthesis, and actinomycin D, which inhibits RNA synthesis.
