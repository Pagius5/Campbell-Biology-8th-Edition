You've been to the California desert several times, and you know what to expect: spiny Joshua trees, scattered shrubs and cactus, a few spidery weeds, lots of gravel, purple hills in the distance, and burning sun. But this time, as you top a familiar hill, brilliant wildflowers carpet the valley before you. You stop and walk among the blooms, and you pick a few that look like yellow daisies. In the distance, a dirt road leads to a cabin.

You feel lucky to see the desert in bloom, and you wonder: Seeds of all these blooming plants must have slept in the ground for several years. What told them to sprout and grow up just now? You have time and a laboratory; you decide to look into it.
