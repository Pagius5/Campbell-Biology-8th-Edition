Haploid gametophytes are the most conspicuous stage in the moss life cycle— the spongy green plants we see covering moist ground or fallen logs. Vase-shaped archegonia, the structures that produce eggs, develop at the tips of female gametophytes. Antheridia, which produce sperm, develop on the tops of male gametophytes. Because the gametophyte stage is haploid, eggs and sperm are produced by mitosis in the archegonia and antheridia.

Sperm swim through moisture from antheridia and fertilize eggs in the archegonia. A diploid fertilized egg, called a zygote, develops into an embryo, which grows into a sporophyte— a tall filament with a sporangium at its tip. The sporophyte obtains its nutrients from the female gametophyte.

In the sporangium, diploid cells undergo meiosis to produce haploid spores. As the sporangium matures and dries out, its lid pops open. Spores spill out and are carried away by the wind.

A spore settles on moist ground, germinates, and develops via mitosis into a threadlike, haploid protonema, which resembles a green alga. The protonema eventually develops into a male or female gametophyte, completing the moss life cycle.
