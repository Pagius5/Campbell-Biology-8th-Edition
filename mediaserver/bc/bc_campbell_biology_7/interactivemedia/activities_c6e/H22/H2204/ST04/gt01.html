As he continued to read and think about Lyell's ideas, Darwin was starting to use a new way to look at the world, one that relied on natural processes that could be observed and measured in the present. Given enough time, these processes could yield the current geology of the Earth. As he traveled in South America, Darwin continued to look for evidence to support this approach of gradualism in geology.

In February 1832, the ship reached Salvador, a coastal town in Brazil. In Darwin's time this town was also known as Bahia and as San Salvador. Brazil had declared its independence from Portugal about ten years earlier.

The area near Salvador is known for its production of sugar cane, which requires a long, frost-free growing season with ample moisture.
